---
title: Run first. Read later
date: 2026-06-16
description: The importance of building CLIs and rannable samples
---

Most web3 projects don’t really lose developers because of the idea itself. More often than not, 
the idea is actually fine, sometimes even strong. The problem shows up much earlier, before anyone 
really gets a chance to understand what they are building on or whether it is worth the effort in 
the first place.

Over the past few years I’ve been building a lot of developer-facing tools across different companies. 
Things like CLIs, sample applications, starter kits, small utilities that are meant to help someone get 
from zero to something working fast. I didn’t start doing this because I thought developer tooling was 
the most in-demand area, but because I kept seeing the same pattern repeat itself over amd over again. 
People would show interest, sometimes even genuine excitement about the tech, and then somewhere between 
“this looks interesting” and “I’ll try it out,” they would quietly drop off. Not because they made a 
decision against it, but because they never actually reached anything concrete enough to evaluate.

When I look back at those moments, the friction was almost always in the same places. A lot of onboarding 
flows assume that a developer is willing to spend time setting things up before they get any real feedback. 
They are asked to read through setup guides, configure environment files, install dependencies, sometimes 
even navigate a dashboard or create an account, all before they can run a single meaningful command. It is 
not that any of these steps are inherently wrong, but when you put them all upfront, the experience becomes 
heavy very quickly. There is a certain psychological weight to it, where even if each individual step is 
reasonable, the total effort feels like a commitment rather than an exploration.

Another thing I noticed is how often teams rely on documentation as the first point of contact. The assumption 
is that people will read, then digest and process, and only then try something out. In practice, most developers 
I’ve worked with behave the opposite way. They want to see something work right away, even if they don’t fully 
understand it yet. The understanding comes after the experience, not before it. When that order is reversed, 
when reading is required before anything runs, a lot of people simply don’t make it through. Not because they 
lack patience, but because there is nothing yet anchoring their attention.

At some point I started shifting how I approached this problem. Instead of trying to improve documentation or 
make onboarding guides more detailed, I focused on removing as much distance as possible between a mere intro and 
a runnable demo. That meant building things like CLIs that you could run with a single command, or sample 
applications that would take someone from an empty folder to a functioning integration in a matter of minutes. 
The important part was not that everything was fully polished, but that something actually works. And developers
love that.

What surprised me was how much that changed the way people responded. When someone sees something working immediately, 
the conversation changes entirely. They stop asking whether it is feasible and start asking how far it can go. 
The documentation still matters (a lot), but it becomes secondary in a way. It turns into something you refer back to 
once you already have basic context on how to work with this particular stack, rather than something you need in order 
to get started. I'm not saying people don't read quickstarts or guides. I'm emphasising on the distinct mindset that 
most software engineers possess.

I saw this quite clearly in a few different cases. At Myria, a TypeScript SDK combined with sample applications 
reduced partner integration time quite significantly, somewhere in the range of 70 to 80 percent. At OMG Network, 
simplifying the interaction layer had a similar effect, cutting integration time by around 30-40%. On TON, a CLI 
I built reduced my own time to first transaction by more than half. In all of these cases, the improvement did not 
come from adding more explanation or more documentation, but from reducing the number of things someone had to do 
before they could see a result.

The more I worked on this, the more I became convinced that the real issue in most onboarding flows is not complexity 
in the technical sense, but delay in feedback. There is usually a moment very early on where a developer decides, 
consciously or not, whether this is something they want to continue with. If that moment is slow, unclear, or requires 
too much setup before anything meaningful happens, most will simply move on. Not because they rejected the idea, 
but because they never reached the point where they could properly engage with it.

And that is the part that still feels important to me. It is not really about better documentation or better UX in 
isolation. It is about how quickly someone can go from curiosity to something real running on their machine, because 
that is usually the moment where the decision actually gets made.
